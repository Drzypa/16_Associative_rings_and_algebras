group of units


Theorem.

The set E of units of a ring R forms a group with respect to ring multiplication.

Proof.  If u and v are two units, then there are the elements r and s of R such that  r⁢u=u⁢r=1  and  s⁢v=v⁢s=1.  Then we get that (s⁢r)⁢(u⁢v)=s⁢(r⁢(u⁢v))=s⁢((r⁢u)⁢v)=s⁢(1⁢v)=s⁢v=1,  similarly  (u⁢v)⁢(s⁢r)=1.  Thus also u⁢v is a unit, which means that E is closed under multiplication.  Because  1∈E  and along with u also its inverse r belongs to E, the set E is a group.

Corollary.  In a commutative ring, a ring product is a unit iff all are units.

The group E of the units of the ring R is called the group of units of the ring.  If R is a field, E is said to be the multiplicative group of the field.

Examples

  1. 1.

    When  R=ℤ, then  E={1,-1}.

  2. 2.

    When  R=ℤ⁢[i],  the ring of Gaussian integers, then  E={1,i,-1,-i}.

  3. 3.

    When  R=ℤ⁢[3], then (http://planetmath.org/UnitsOfQuadraticFields)  E={±(2+3)n⋮n∈ℤ}.

  4. 4.

    When  R=K⁢[X]  where K is a field, then  E=K∖{0}.

  5. 5.

    When  R={0+ℤ, 1+ℤ,…,m-1+ℤ}  is the residue class ring modulo m, then  E consists of the prime classes modulo m, i.e. the residue classes l+ℤ satisfying  gcd⁡(l,m)=1.
